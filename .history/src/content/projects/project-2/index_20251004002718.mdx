This project began with a simple question: *what if we could stand inside an AI-generated alternate version of a place?*  

Most generative image tools give you a single snapshot. I wanted something more immersive: an **iterative workflow for creating 360° panoramas** that let you look around in all directions, as if you were inside a “what-if machine.” The aim was to explore applications across **urban planning, education, and cultural tourism** — areas where immersion really matters.

---

## From Samples to Panoramas  

The system starts with a set of about 50 sample images. These act as seeds for generating spherical projections. A user can view the pano in-browser, then refine it with prompts — for example, changing the season, adding street furniture, or reimagining the architecture.  

Each step builds on the previous panorama, producing a chain of iterations. The workflow looks something like this:

The result isn’t just a static render, but an **interactive panorama** that can be nudged, reshaped, and reimagined.

## What Emerged  

Three domains stood out where this kind of workflow feels especially valuable:  

### 1. Urban Planning & Architecture  
A local council could preview “before–after” designs: bike lanes added, trees planted, lighting shifted to evening. Instead of flat diagrams, residents could *stand in the middle of the street* and judge if the redesign feels right.  

**Prompt example:**  
*“Take this panorama of Main Street. Add a protected bike lane, plant trees along the curb, and render the scene at golden-hour sunset.”*  

---

### 2. Education & Storytelling  
History teachers could drop students inside the past. Trafalgar Square in 1850: gas lamps instead of LEDs, horse-drawn carriages instead of buses, a coal-smog haze in the air. Geography students could explore glacial valleys or speculative climate futures.  

**Prompt example:**  
*“Using this panorama of Trafalgar Square, reconstruct it as in 1850: gas lamps, horse-drawn carriages, pedestrians in Victorian clothing, and a smoky haze.”*  

---

### 3. Tourism & Cultural Heritage  
Tourism boards could showcase the same landmark across seasons: a Kyoto shrine glowing red in autumn, blanketed by snow in winter, or drenched in monsoon rain. Travelers don’t just see the place — they *feel themselves inside it.*  

**Prompt example:**  
*“Transform this panorama of Fushimi Inari Shrine into an autumn scene: red-orange maple leaves, fallen leaves on the path, soft sunlight through the torii gates.”*  

---

## Lessons and Next Steps  

The exercise revealed that panoramas amplify the impact of generative imagery. Because you’re inside them, small changes — a tree here, different lighting there — feel embodied, not abstract. That makes them powerful for engagement, teaching, and imagination.  

Challenges remain. Panos can warp under heavy edits; iterative prompts sometimes drift too far from realism. Anchoring generation more tightly to depth maps or existing 3D scans could improve consistency.  

Still, even with these quirks, the project shows how **AI can generate immersive counterfactuals of place**. Not just predicting what is, but letting us explore what was, what could be, and what we imagine.
